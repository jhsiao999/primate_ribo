Goal

Here we consider a set of genes with sig. RNA-protein interaction and investigate the contribution of buffering at the translation level to the protein level. Two approaches are discussed

## from the object eSetRRP.log2 in eSetRRP.rda
load( file.path(rdadir,"eSetRRP.rda") )

eSet.temp <- eSetRRP.log2[ ,eSetRRP.log2$species!="rhesus"]

fc.mat <- lapply( seq_along( c("human","chimp") ), function(i) {
  ii <- eSet.temp$species == c("human","chimp")[i]
  eSet.tt <- eSet.temp[, ii]
  emat <- lapply( seq_along( c("ribo","rna","protein") ), function(j) {
    jj <- eSet.tt$seqData == c("ribo","rna","protein")[j]
    rowMeans( exprs(eSet.tt[, jj] ), na.rm=TRUE )
  })
  emat <- do.call(cbind, emat)
  colnames(emat) <- c("ribo","rna","protein")
  return( data.frame(emat) )
})
names(fc.mat) <- c("human","chimp")

dmat_unnormed <- data.frame( ribo = fc.mat$human$ribo-fc.mat$chimp$ribo,

Interaction appoach: take the set of genes with sig. RNA-protein interaction, select a subset of genes with sig. RNA-ribo interaction. Then, we divided the genes into two categories

* Buffered at the protein level: abs(RNA fold change) > abs(Protein fold change)
* Non-Buffered at the protein level: abs(RNA fold change) < abs(Protein fold change)

RNA ~ Protein

Among 2877 genes that are neither enhanced nor attenuated at the protein level compared to the RNA level (RNA ~ Protein), we identified 375 genes with significant RNA-Ribo interaction and further classifed these genes as follows

  • 129 genes are buffered at the translation level relative to the transcript level, and

  • 246 genes are consistent with a pattern of non-buffered genes at the tranlsation level relative to the transcript level.

Buffered at translation

Among the 129 genes that were buffered at the translation level relative to the transcript level, we tested the null hypothesis that these genes are as likely to be buffered as to be non-buffered at the protein level relative to the transcript level (RNA-Protein). If this null hypothesis were rejected and the number of genes that were buffered at the protein layer is larger than the number of non-buffered genes at the protein layer, then we say that buffering at the translation level is more likey to co-occur with buffering at the protein level than with non-buffering at the protein level.

Not buffered at translation

Among the 246 genes that were not buffered at translation, we tested the null hypothesis that these genes are as likely to be non-buffered as to be buffered at the protein layer. If this null hypothesis were rejected and the number of genes that were not buffered at the protein layer is more than those buffered at the protein layer, then we say that genes that are non-buffered at the translation level are more likely to be non-buffered at the protein level than to be buffered at the protein level.

RNA > Protein

Among the genes that are buffered at the protein level compared to the RNA level (RNA > protein), we are interested in the number of genes with buffered translation. In other words, we ask whether genes that were buffered at the protein level were more likely to be enriched with genes buffered at the translation level.

For the hypothesis testing, we compute the expected proportion of genes enriched at the translation level among the genes that were buffered at the protein level in the set of genes where there is no significant interaction between RNA and protein (2877 genes).
